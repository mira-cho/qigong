The Thread — A Practice
Qigong after Master Zhongxian Wu
35–45 minutes · practice outdoors when possible
Arriving —
noticing you are already here

Before any movement: the practice is to stop performing and actually land in the body, in this place, in this moment.

Ziran 自然 — self-so-ness. The wood grain is already there. You are learning to see it.
How
Feet shoulder-width apart, knees softly unlocked. Imagine a golden thread lifting from the crown of the head. Let everything that isn't needed drop away — jaw, shoulders, belly. Eyes half-closed, gaze soft and low. Simply be here, doing nothing at all.
Why
Wuji is the primordial state before Yin and Yang divide. Master Wu teaches that by returning to Wuji, we reconnect with our original nature — the self before habit, ambition, and thought.
The thread
This is the same quality as pausing before picking up a chisel. Or the breath before a brush touches paper. Or the moment in yoga before you adjust anything. The practice is learning to carry this quality of arrival into everything else.
3–5 min natural breath, unmanaged
How
Let the shaking begin at the knees and rise without direction through hips, belly, spine, arms, jaw. Low effort, total permission. Like a dog emerging from water. Let sound come if it wants to.
Why
Rooted in shamanic practice, Dou awakens energy and consciousness in the body, opening the energy gates and meridians. It breaks up the body's accumulated holding — the tension that arrived before you noticed it.
The thread
Notice what the shaking dislodges. The tension you didn't know you were carrying. The Qigong master, the wood carver finding the grain, and the yogi finding ease in a pose — all are doing the same thing: releasing the unnecessary so the natural can move.
2–3 min natural, let movement disrupt it
Moving —
following rather than forcing

The movements are slow enough that you can notice where you are pushing instead of following. That noticing is the practice.

Wu Wei 無為 — effortless action. The Zhuangzi's cook never dulls his blade because he follows what is already open.
How
From Wuji, inhale as both palms float upward — leading with the thumbnails — arms rising overhead, fingers pointing to sky. Rise slightly onto the balls of the feet. Exhale, arms lower, palms press gently toward earth, heels return. 9 rounds, very slowly.
Why
Opens the central channel (Chong Mai) and harmonizes all three Dantians. A direct embodiment of the Daoist cosmological axis: heaven above, earth below, human being as the living bridge.
The thread
When you are outside: you are not imitating this — you actually are it. The sky is above you. The ground is beneath your feet. The practice is just making that real, instead of theoretical. The same truth lives in a tree growing, in a lathe spiral going up and up.
5 min inhale rising · exhale lowering
How
Stand or sit. Place the tongue lightly on the roof of the mouth behind the teeth — this connects the two main channels. Inhale, awareness up the spine from the base to the crown. Exhale, awareness flows down the front midline — throat, heart, belly, back to the base. Don't force the sensation. Follow what is already there.
Why
The Cosmic Orbit circulates Qi through the Governing and Conception Vessels — the body's two primary meridians. The tongue-to-palate connection (the magpie bridge) completes the circuit. Foundation of Daoist inner alchemy.
The thread
The grain in wood spirals through the whole tree. The breath in the body has its own circuit. Yoga asana at its best is the same: finding the body's natural pathway rather than imposing a shape onto it. These are all the same teaching in different materials.
5–7 min inhale up the back · exhale down the front
How
From Wuji, inhale as both arms open wide to the sides, palms forward — expansive, unhurried. Feel the ribs and the space between them. Exhale as arms gather inward, hands resting just below the navel, palms toward the body. Imagine drawing luminous energy from the whole field around you into your center. 9 rounds.
Why
From Master Wu's Shamanic Tiger Qigong (Laohu Gong). Activates the extraordinary meridians and stores cultivated Qi in the lower Dantian. The tiger represents raw natural power that has been refined — not tamed, but integrated.
The thread
The craftsperson's attention gathers like this: wide at first, reading the whole piece, then narrowing into the specific place where the chisel meets the grain. Yoga does the same in a wide opening pose before folding into stillness. The movement teaches the rhythm of encounter.
5 min inhale opening · exhale gathering
How
One hand rises overhead, palm pressing skyward. The other sinks beside the hip, palm pressing toward earth. Feel the long, gentle traction through the body. Hold 3–5 breaths. Return to center and reverse. 9 rounds each side, slowly.
Why
Yin and Yang are not opposites in conflict — they are the dynamic tension that makes everything alive. This movement embodies that tension in the body: one hand ascends, one descends, and you are the living space between them.
The thread
The green wood curl on the lathe — it spirals because of the tension between the spinning wood and the still edge of the tool. The curl is the meeting of opposite forces. This movement is training you to inhabit that kind of creative tension instead of resolving it too quickly.
4–5 min inhale into the stretch · exhale softening
Closing —
carrying it forward

The closing is not putting down the practice. It is asking: where does this quality want to go next?

Pu 樸 — the uncarved block. Not empty but full of all possible forms. You return to it so you can act from it.
How
Both hands rest over the lower Dantian — right hand on skin, left over right (reverse for women). Circle the hands slowly: 9 times clockwise, gathering inward. Then 9 times counter-clockwise, releasing any excess. Gradually reduce the circles until the hands are still. 3 long, quiet breaths.
Why
Without this sealing, the Qi raised during practice disperses. The closing consolidates the practice into the body rather than leaving it suspended in the air.
The thread
A carving session or a yoga practice also needs a close. Not cleaning up the shavings — but the internal moment of setting down the tool with intention, rather than just stopping. The practice is complete. You are changed by it.
3 min natural, quieting
How
Inhale slowly. On a long exhale: open the mouth and release a soft, low HAAAA from the chest — without performance, just release. Repeat 3 times. Then: a humming MMMM that vibrates the skull, 3 times. Finally: a silent, slow exhale through slightly parted lips, as though cooling something.
Why
Sound (Jue 訣) is one of the three essential practices of Master Wu's shamanic tradition alongside mudras and talismans. Healing sounds clear stuck Qi from the organ systems. The HAAAA releases the heart and lungs. The hum resonates the upper spirit gates.
The thread
The moment when a piece of green wood splits cleanly along the grain — there is often a sound. Can you receive it the way you receive the HAAAA? Not as a performance, not as an outcome, but as an event that simply happened because the conditions were right.
2–3 min long exhales, sound as vehicle
How
Return to Wuji standing. Don't immediately plan what comes next. Feel the quality present in the body right now — alive, quiet, here. Inwardly ask without urgency: is there a craft waiting? A yoga mat? A walk? A conversation? Let the answer come from the body rather than the schedule. Bow gently toward the ground and sky before moving.
Why
This is the core transfer. The Qigong practice has no value if it stays quarantined in practice time. The closing invites the quality cultivated — presence, naturalness, following rather than forcing — to migrate into whatever comes next.
The thread
This is where the tool acquisition disappears and the spoon wall disappears. You are not asking "what should I do" — you are noticing what wants to happen through you, the way a river doesn't decide to flow downhill. That noticing, right now, is the Dao at work.
2–3 min natural, unhurried
